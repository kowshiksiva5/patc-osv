Technical evidence

What the model proves, and what it still has to earn.

The current canvas is a concept replay, not a claimed field result. The pilot exists to replace hand-tuned assumptions with calibrated Bengaluru observations and repeatable baseline comparisons.

State variables rho, q, Pi_m, wait, spillback risk

Objective Reduce sector delay without starving cross movement

The current replay scores queue pressure, wait, synthetic downstream risk, and minimum green; the field pilot adds pedestrian, emergency, stale-data, and operator fallback constraints.

Baseline Fixed-time and actuated comparison

Every replay should produce a before/after trace: current plan, recommended phase, expected effect, confidence, and failure reason.

Calibration Field labels before field control

Queue length, discharge rate, spillback, empty-green, phase state, and weather/event tags become the acceptance dataset.

Current limit Synthetic context pressure

Neighbor-node pressure in the demo is synthetic until replaced with measured queues, occupancy, and downstream capacity labels.

Public benefit

The visible promise is fewer wasted stops.

PATC is built to be felt by ordinary commuters, but it should earn trust before touching a live signal. The first product is a shadow-mode replay that shows whether smarter timing would reduce red-light waits without making cross traffic unsafe.

For drivers

Smoother movement

The goal is fewer stop-start waves across a linked sector, not a single lucky green at one pole.

For air

Less idle waste

Reduced waiting and hard braking should lower wasted fuel and emissions once field baselines prove the effect.

For operators

Explainable decisions

Every recommendation comes with a reason, confidence score, safety fallback, and replay trace.

For cities

Replay before rollout

Peak, rain, school, weekend, event, and seasonal patterns are tested before any live deployment request.

What gets measuredQueue length, discharge rate, phase timing, empty-green time, rain/event tags, and downstream blocking.
How it stays privateNo raw video by default; labels focus on traffic state, not people or plates, and complaint-triggered deletion is immediate.
Who stays in controlTraffic operators keep authority through minimum greens, pedestrian windows, emergency priority, stale-data stops, and manual override.
When it scalesOnly after replay beats fixed-time and actuated baselines across normal, rainy, school, weekend, event, and seasonal traffic.

From demo to street

A practical path from replay to a real city sector.

Before commuters see a change at the signal, the system has to observe real traffic, calibrate the physics, replay alternate green windows, and pass a human-reviewed safety gate.

01Observe

Build clean site-hour data across peak, off-peak, rain, school, weekend, event, and seasonal variation.

02Calibrate

Fit density, discharge, startup loss, and spillback behavior to the chosen sector.

03Replay

Compare PATC recommendations against fixed-time and actuated baselines before any field control.

04Pilot

Move to supervised live testing only after operators can audit the decision, risk, and fallback trace.

Rollout

Prove it in Bengaluru traffic. Reuse it across any congested corridor.

Bengaluru is the proving ground because the traffic is dense, heterogeneous, and unforgiving. Once PATC can explain and improve a messy local sector in replay, the same operating layer can be rolled out to other congested corridors.
