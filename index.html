Most signal upgrades start by counting vehicles. PATC starts with a harder question: what will happen if this phase changes now, and how confident are we in that answer?

Founder backstory

Two engineers got stuck in the same broken control loop.

1. Bangalore commute painThey kept seeing empty greens, overflowing approaches, and rigid cycles that ignored live demand.
2. Naive idea rejectedEarly thinking was “count vehicles better.” They learned count is not control.
3. Field reality hitRain, glare, occlusion, downstream blockage, and operator trust matter as much as algorithms.
4. Shadow-mode insightBefore live control, the system should observe, recommend, explain, and compare.

The validation founder

A mechanical engineer from IIT Hyderabad, now working in a heavy-industry role, brings testing discipline from industrial systems where validation, vendor coordination, and capital decisions around large deployments cannot be hand-waved.

His instinct was to treat traffic as a safety-critical control system: define the baseline, name the failure modes, test in simulation, then move toward field readiness only after repeatable evidence.

The execution founder

An electronics and software engineer with roughly three years of project execution experience owns the sensing, embedded prototype, controller logic, and operator dashboard.

His early lesson was blunt: vehicle detection is not traffic control. Counting cars is easier than making a safe timing decision under rain, glare, occlusion, phase history, and downstream blockage.

“The first version should earn trust in shadow mode. Public infrastructure should not be controlled by a black box that cannot explain itself.”

OSV fellowship year

One year to become field-ready.

  1. Months 1-2Lock one Bangalore junction candidate, build baseline dataset, document fixed-time waste and failure modes.
  2. Months 3-4Calibrate a one-junction simulator that predicts queue growth under uncertain arrivals and compares timing recommendations against fixed-time baselines.
  3. Months 5-6Launch shadow-mode dashboard: observe, recommend, explain, and compare against the current signal plan.
  4. Months 7-9Run statistical validation across synthetic and observed scenarios; publish a measurable one-junction report.
  5. Months 10-12Build embedded prototype, hardware-in-loop demo, and traffic-department-ready pilot protocol.

OSV application core

Answers that match the team and the project.

What are you working on?

We are building PATC, a traffic signal control layer that observes live queues, models uncertainty, and recommends green-time changes before a junction wastes another signal cycle.

The first product is a shadow-mode system for one Bangalore junction: it watches the current signal plan, predicts near-future queue pressure, and produces explainable timing recommendations with confidence and fallback rules.

Why this team?

The team combines two skills that traffic pilots usually need and rarely get together: industrial validation discipline and embedded/software execution. One founder brings IIT Hyderabad mechanical engineering and heavy-industry validation/testing experience; the other can build the sensor-controller-dashboard stack.

The novel part is not claiming “AI traffic lights.” The novel part is uncertainty-aware control: PATC makes a recommendation, explains the evidence, shows confidence, and remains useful in shadow mode before it earns live control.

One-year success

Success in one year is a working shadow pilot for one junction: live or replayed traffic observation, a calibrated simulator, a dashboard that traffic engineers can inspect, a mock controller, and a report showing where the current signal plan wastes green time or creates queue spillback.

Beyond our wildest dreams, PATC becomes the adaptive layer cities can test safely before procurement: a standard way to move Indian intersections from fixed schedules to explainable, probabilistic control.

Failure mode

This fails if it becomes another impressive traffic demo that no operator trusts. Specific risks are bad data access, camera failure in rain/night, local optimization that worsens the next junction, slow government pilots, and an interface that feels like a black box.

Our mitigation is to begin in shadow mode, publish baselines, keep manual override, show confidence on every recommendation, and prove one measurable junction before claiming citywide impact.
